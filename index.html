Research Interests

    My research area during graduation is focussed primarily on game-theoretic treatments within the framework of epidemiological settings. I have worked on the containment of the spread of infectious diseases during a pandemic, by integrating epidemiological mathematical models with human decision-making. To this end, I have considered a large population of decision-makers, in which the individuals are modeled to be non-myopic agents. Prior works have mainly modeled the decision-makers to be myopic, who consider only instantaneous rewards while choosing their actions. However, in reality the picture is quite different. For instance, people choose vaccination as their strategy even though the immediate cost is high, but it protects them from getting infected in the long-run. By modeling individuals as forward-looking, and applying Dynamic Population Game Theory, the Nash strategies are characterized, and optimal policies are determined for various parameter regimes.

    In addition, I have also considered a partial information based environment, in which agents are unaware of their exact infection status. They rely upon signals that the sender disseminates. This problem has been solved within the framework of Bayesian Persuasion, to obtain the optimal signaling schemes of the sender to minimize infection level. I have also been involved in learning-based control, and have developed skills in using optimal control solvers (such as the QuITO, BOCOP etc.) to solve finite-time horizon optimal control problems of complex dynamical systems, in a direct manner.


    The primary questions answered are as follows.

  • What are the Nash strategies chosen by the forward looking individuals in a large population under different parameter regimes, and how they influence the spread of epidemics?
  • In what way does lockdown strategies imposed on recovered individuals influences the peak infection level?
  • What is the role of information in epidemic games, when individuals are unaware of their infection status?
  • Can we reduce infection prevelance simply by modulating the information transmitted to individuals having partial knowledge about their state? If so, what is the optimal signal sent to minimize infection spread?
